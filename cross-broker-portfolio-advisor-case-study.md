### Problem
I noticed that serious retail investors in India almost always use two brokers—Groww for its simplicity in mutual funds, and Zerodha for its trading depth. But neither platform talks to the other, forcing users to maintain manual Excel sheets just to understand their own net worth and true asset allocation. Without a unified view, they make skewed portfolio decisions based on fragmented data. That blind spot is exactly the problem I wanted to solve.

### Approach
My initial instinct was aggregation through visualization, but I quickly realized the real pain point wasn't presentation—it was interpretation. Investors didn't just want to see their scattered assets; they wanted to know what to do with them. That led me to prioritize an aggregation-first model powered by AI rather than trying to build a completely new brokerage from scratch. The core insight was that the Indian market doesn't need another place to buy stocks; it needs an intelligence layer sitting on top of existing accounts to analyze overlaps, flag risky exposures, and suggest rebalancing. I brought in an LLM not to be a chatty assistant, but to act as a personalized financial analyst that actually understands the context of a user's total net worth.

### Key Decisions
The most critical technical decision was forcing all mathematical calculations through a deterministic backend instead of letting the LLM handle the math. The alternative was relying on the AI to calculate portfolio weights and returns, but LLMs are prone to mathematical errors. By using the AI solely for natural language parsing and routing the actual math to a traditional calculator, I ensured absolute trust in the numbers. Another major choice was relying strictly on the official Account Aggregator framework rather than scraping data or using unofficial broker APIs. While scraping would have allowed us to launch faster and support more obscure brokers immediately, the Account Aggregator route guaranteed data security, regulatory compliance, and zero broken connections when a broker updates their interface. Finally, I intentionally designed friction into the trade execution flow rather than offering one-click approvals. The alternative of a seamless, instantaneous trade felt slicker, but when an AI recommends reallocating thousands of rupees, users need a moment of cognitive pause to review the underlying logic. That friction builds long-term trust and prevents panicked or accidental trades.

### What's Next
For the initial launch, I strictly scoped out family portfolio tracking and automated tax harvesting to get the core individual experience right. However, V2 will definitely introduce multi-PAN household views. I realized during research that Indian investors rarely invest in isolation; they manage accounts for their spouses and parents and treating those as separate silos defeats the entire purpose of total wealth aggregation. Once the household view is stable, the next logical step is building a tax-loss harvesting engine. Right now, the AI can tell you your portfolio is overexposed, but eventually, it needs to tell you exactly which specific lots to sell before March 31st to optimize capital gains tax. Those features will evolve the product from a smart analyzer into an indispensable wealth management tool.
